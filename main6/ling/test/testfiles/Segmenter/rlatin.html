LATIN: the father of the Romance languages

BASIC INFORMATION
Latin, the father of the Romance languages. When we imagine Latin, we see a dead language whose speakers long ago loved nothing but grammar and world-domination. Latin; however, is far from dead, and, I assure you, some of those children hated their grammar excercises as much as the average high school student of the language. All in all, Latin is the official language of the Vatican, and many instructors hold onto it as though it were still very much alive (and, I assure you, it is). Of all other major Romance Languages, Latin most closely resembles Italian when written and Spanish when spoken (although there are those who hold to the Italian pronunciation of Church Latin, a non-traditional [i.e. contra-Classical] pronunciation of the language). Some characteristics of Latin that you will notice are that words tend to be inflected (endings determine meaning), and that the language has many double-consonants of a single letter (such as annus [year],  esse [to be]).

As a rule, the stress falls one syllable back from the last if that syllable is long (called the penultimate). Clamáre, then, is pronounced "cluh-mAH-reh", with a great amount of weight on the second syllable. If this syllable is not long, the stress falls on the syllable preceeding that (such as fáblua, pronounced fAH-boo-luh). T

Exempla (examples):

When reading Latin, the vowel length will often not be so conveniently marked (just as an English speaker would not use a mark to indicate the difference of "a" in hamburger and date).

BASIC GRAMMAR
Latin uses many words and grammatical styles that a speaker of any other Romance Language would recognize.

The Latin noun has either a masculine, feminine, or neuter gender. In the singular, masculine tends to end in -us, feminine in -a, and neuter in -um. Words in -tió/-sió (related to Spanish -ción/-sión, Portuguese -ção/-são, French -tion/-sion, Italian -zione/-sione, Romanian -t¸ie [sometimes -t¸iune]/-sie [sometimes -siune], and Catalan -ció/-sió) are feminine, just as in those languages. In forming the plural, an -us becomes -í, an -a becomes -ae, and an -um becomes -a. These; however, apply only to the nominative form of the noun (i.e. when the noun acts as the subject).

The articles (a/an/the) are not present in Latin, so the English speaker often adds them in translating.

Here are the basic Latin declensions for nouns in -us (generally masculine), -a (generally feminine), and -um (generally neuter):

Accusative -um -am -um

Thus come the "horrors" of the Latin declensions:

The nominative is the subject form, the noun that directs the sentence, and which the verb accompanies (le nom is French word for the noun or the name). The vocative (Latin vox means voice) indicates only a calling out, and is quite often put to use after Ó! (= Oh!). The accusative receives the action of the verb or of a preposition (hence, it is accused). The genitive shows the possession of the noun (generation, genetics, and gene all entail some form of belonging). The dative is an indirect objective case, delineating the "to" or "for" aspect of a noun. The ablative is simply a left-over case used with a few prepositions as well as for some other reasons.

If I were to run a hot dog stand, look at how I might use the cases in English:

Nominative:   The hot dog stand gets good business. (subject)
Vocative:        OH, HOT DOG STAND! (addressed)
Accusative:    I run the hot dog stand. (object of verb)
Genitive:         The hot dog stand's business / The business of the hot dog stand. (possession)
Dative:            I give time and effort to the hot dog stand. (direction to/for)

The Latin verb follows the same basic principles as those of other Romance languages languages, so don't be surprised if you recognize a verb, verb form, or usage of a verb as being similar or exactly like that of Spanish, French, etc. As in any Romance Language, the present indicative tense of the verb occurs in most situations where English would use the present participle (in Latin, for example, fábuló means "I speak" or "I am speaking). This is notable for those who do not have much experience in Romance (the languages, that is), but for those who do, it shouldn't come as a surprise.

Italian has three verb terminations ("infinitive endings"): -áre, -ére, -íre, and -ere. Here are the regular present indicative tense conjugations for each of the infinitive endings, including sample verbs:

  -áre fábuláre (to speak) -ére monére (to warn) -íre scíre (to know) -ere legere (to read)
egó (I) -ó fábuló -eó moneó -ió sció -ó legó
tú (you)* -ás fábulás -és monés -ís scís -is legis
is (he) -at fábulat -et monet -it scit -it legit
ea (she) -at fábulat -et monet -it scit -it legit
nós (we) -ámus fábulámus -émus monémus -ímus scímus -imus legimus
vós (all of you)* -átis fábulátis -étis monétis -ítis scítis -itis legitis
ií (they) -ant fábulant -ent monent -iunt sciunt -unt legunt

* Tú is used for familiar/informal situations when addressing a single person, vós (or Vós)  for more formal ones. Vós is the plural of tú, but may, in certain cases, act as the singular formal form.

Hispánum loquerisne?, for example, translates Do you speak Spanish? (note that "-ne" attached to the verb loqueris, akin to fábulás [you speak]), indicates that this is a question, for there is no other question word such as "What...?" or "Who...?") .

Adjectives agree with the noun, and usually follow them: vir félix ([a/the] happy man). In fact, they are declined just as a Latin noun, so there is no confusion. An adverb is formed by an -ius stem rather than the regular, nominative stem (-us, -a, -um, et cetera).

BASIC WORDS AND PHRASES
These are some of the more common phrases you would hear protruding from the mouth of a Latin speaker on a daily basis, so, if you wish to become acquainted with the language, these are a great way to start:

Salve! = Goodday!
Salve! = Hello!
Quómodo valés? = How are you?, How's it going?
Quómodo valétis? = How are you? (formal)
Bene. = (I'm) well.
Male. = Badly.
Admodum bene. = So-so.
Multius... = Very...
Quid est nomen tibi/vóbis? = What's your name? / What are your names?
Et tú? = And you?
Et vós? = And you? (formal)
Mé placet té cognoscere. = Pleased to meet you.
Sis. = Please.
Mé excusáte. = Excuse me.
...est = Here is...
...est = There is...
...sunt = There are...
Ita. = Yes.
Nón. = No.
Grátiás (dó). = Thank you.
Salutatió. = You're welcome.
Quam...? = What?
Cúr? = Why?
Quota hóra est? = What time is it?
Vale! / Ave! = Goodbye!, See you later!
